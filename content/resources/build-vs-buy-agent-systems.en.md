Every enterprise evaluating agent systems faces a build-vs-buy decision. The honest answer: it depends on your engineering capability, your timeline, and how core the coordination problem is to your competitive advantage.

Build in-house when: you have a strong AI/ML engineering team (5+ engineers with LLM deployment experience), your coordination workflows are highly unique to your industry or organization, and you're willing to invest 6–12 months before seeing production value. The advantage: complete control and deep organizational knowledge. The risk: most internal AI teams are optimized for analytics and modeling, not production systems engineering.

Use a platform when: your coordination needs are generic (e.g., standard approval routing, report compilation), your systems are mainstream (Salesforce, SAP, Oracle), and you're optimizing for speed over customization. Several agent platforms now handle standard enterprise workflows well. The advantage: fast time-to-value. The risk: limited customization and dependency on the vendor's roadmap.

Engage a specialist firm when: your coordination problem is complex and industry-specific, you need production deployment within 8–12 weeks, and you want to own the system after delivery. This is our model. We diagnose the specific coordination bottleneck, design a custom agent architecture, build and deploy it, and transfer full ownership to your team. The advantage: specialist speed with permanent ownership. The risk: you need to invest in internal capability to maintain and extend the system after handoff.

The worst option: buying a general-purpose AI platform and hoping the coordination problem solves itself. It won't. Coordination automation requires understanding the specific operational topology — the real handoffs, the actual decision patterns, the existing system landscape. No platform can substitute for that understanding.
