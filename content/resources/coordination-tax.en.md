In every industrial enterprise above a certain complexity threshold, a hidden cost structure emerges: the coordination tax. Engineers compile reports the systems already know. Managers relay information between departments because data doesn't flow between tools. Procurement approvals stall for days while someone manually assembles context from five systems.

This isn't a people problem. It's an architectural defect. The coordination layer — how signals get aggregated, how decisions get routed, how execution gets triggered across teams — was never designed. It evolved organically, stitched together by email threads, WeChat groups, and shared spreadsheets.

The cost is enormous and invisible. We've audited operations where managers spend 8–15 hours per week on pure coordination overhead: status compilation, approval chasing, cross-system data gathering. Multiply that across an organization with dozens of managers and hundreds of engineers, and you're looking at thousands of hours per month consumed by work that creates zero value.

Agent systems don't eliminate coordination. They automate the mechanical parts — the signal aggregation, the context assembly, the routing — so humans can focus on the parts that actually require judgment: interpreting exceptions, making trade-off decisions, managing relationships.

The question isn't whether your organization has a coordination tax. It does. The question is whether it's large enough to justify an architectural intervention.
