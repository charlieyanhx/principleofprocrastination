The failure rate of enterprise AI projects is well-documented: depending on whose numbers you trust, 70–85% of projects never reach production. The standard explanations — bad data, lack of executive sponsorship, unclear ROI — are correct but incomplete. They describe symptoms, not root causes.

The deeper problem is architectural. Most enterprise AI projects start at the wrong layer. They begin with analytics ('let's predict demand') or with chat interfaces ('let's build a copilot for our team') — both of which are valuable, but neither of which addresses the coordination bottleneck that actually consumes the most operational time.

Prediction without action is just a dashboard. A chatbot that answers questions doesn't fix the fact that approvals take three days because context is scattered across five systems. The high-leverage intervention point isn't generating more information — it's automating how information flows between people, systems, and decisions.

This is why agent systems represent a different category of enterprise AI. They don't sit on top of workflows as an analytics or interface layer. They operate within the workflow itself: aggregating signals, routing decisions, triggering execution. The value isn't insight — it's operational throughput.

The projects that reach production share three characteristics: they target a specific, measurable coordination bottleneck; they integrate with existing systems rather than replacing them; and they start small enough to validate within 8–12 weeks. Everything else is a research project with a corporate budget.
